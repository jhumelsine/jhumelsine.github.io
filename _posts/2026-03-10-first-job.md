# Lather, Rinse, Repeat
For the most part, just about everything I described in obtaining an [Internship](TBD) (TBD) applies for your first job as well.
You'll still need a [Résumé](TBD) (TBD), [Elevator Pitch](TBD) (TBD), [Company Search](TBD) (TBD) and [Interviews](TBD) (TBD).

The main differences between obtaining an internship offer and a first job offer are:
* The recruiters and interviewers will be more discerning and challenging. This is no longer a 3-month committment. This is a committment that tends to last for years.
* [Likeability](TBD) (TBD) may be even more important, since you'll definitely be working with a team rather than being sequestered with other interns working on your own projects.
* You may need to put more consideration into your own [Decision](TBD) (TBD), since this is no longer a 3-month committment for you either. You will spend most of your week at work. Make sure you choose based upon attributes important to you, such as: location, company reputation, company culture, compensation, benefits, growth opportunity, etc. Each person will evaluate decisions based upon their own preferences. Different people with the same offers will often reach different conclusions.

Your first job is often the most difficult one to get, since most new software developers have limited practical experience.
Once you have an employment track record with a year or two of practical experience, companies are more willing to consider you as a candidate.

Ideally, most graduates will have a job lined up upon graduation.
But much like the [Alternative Path](TBD) (TBD) situation, in which an internship was not obtained, not everyone lands a job before graduation.

<img src="/assets/RyanKanbanBoard.jpg" alt="Kanban Board"  width = "50%" align="right" style="padding: 35px;">

My son did not have a job offer when he graduated with his Mechanical Engineering degrees several years ago.
He moved home in the interim.
He and I executed the plan I described in [Finding Your Next Job](https://jhumelsine.github.io/2024/08/11/jobsearch.html#finding-your-next-job).
I put a bit of an [_Agile_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agile_software_development) spin on it via a simple [Kanban Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanban_board) made of Post-It Notes, Sharpies, Painter's Tape and a sheet of Costco cardboard.

Most days we reviewed his status and set up a plan for the next day.
I encouraged him to do three things each day in his job search.
We reviewed his résumé and ran through a few mock interviews.
He landed his first job about two months after graduation.

When my son graduated in 2022, his Computer Science classmates were being scooped by companies with wild abandon.
My wife wondered whether he had chosen the wrong major with Mechanical Engineering.
I reassured her that the software industry is cyclical.
Four years later, as of the time of this blog, the momentum has shifted.
Generative/Agentic AI is shaking the foundations of software development making that first job upon graduation for Computer Science majors more difficult than other years.
I'm sure the pendulum will swing back once more.

# Onboarding
Academia does a good job teaching you [Computer Science](https://jhumelsine.github.io/2026/02/11/computer-science-software-engineering.html#computer-science-problems), but it doesn't do a great job of preparing you for your career as a [Software Engineer](https://jhumelsine.github.io/2026/02/11/computer-science-software-engineering.html#software-engineering-problems).
An internship introduces most students to the industry, but it's often a sheltered introduction.

The first job can be a bit disorienting.
If I had to reduce my advice to new Software Engineers, it would be this: **Show Up!**

_80% of success is showing up._ — Woody Allen

Your main responsibility is to show up.
They will have a plan for you.
Follow it.

You're first few days to weeks will be spent [Onboarding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onboarding), which is _Company Orientation_, which is crash course in the company.
Onboarding varies among companies.
Some onboarding may not be much more than a compliance few videos you need to watch on your computer.
Other onboardng sessions may be several days long and be so intense and overwhelming that it feels like you're drinking from a fire hose.

Here are a few topics that are fairly common:
* The company's history and culture
* The company's business domain
* The company's main products
* The company's internal procedures and paperwork, especially with respect to HR:
    * Payment schedule
    * Medical
    * Vacation/PTO policy
    * Retirement plan
    * Etc.

This type of onboarding is usually generic and applies to most employees.
Developer onboarding often continues in learning the tech stack, the product architecture, design and some implementation.
It may include exercises to make changes so that you can learn more about the codebase by doing.

For some companies they may send new developers to extensive training, much like a software bootcamp.

Listen to the first segment of the [Soft Skills Podcast](https://softskills.audio/) episode about [Onboarding New College Grads](https://softskills.audio/2025/12/22/episode-492-fresh-grads-and-startups-or-the-goog/).
While the segment is framed from the point of view of how the company can better onboard new college grads, I think there are some insights for the new college grads as well.

**NOTE:** Also consider subscribing to the [Soft Skills Podcast](https://softskills.audio/).
It's more about the _soft skills_ one needs to be a great software developer rather than the technical skills.
It's a weekly 30-minute podcast and quite entertaining.
It's one of my favorites.

Here are somethings to consider doing during onboarding and beyond:
* Go into the office as long as it's not a remote job. You'll pick up much more in person than working remotely.
* Learn the tech stack and other related technology.
* Learn the tools they use to track and document their work.
* Learn the business.
* Read their documentation, which is hopefully online.
* Find a mentor if one has not been assigned to you. Hopefully one will be assigned. Otherwise, you may need to ask your manager for some assistance.

<img src="https://i1.pickpik.com/photos/576/954/363/underwater-sea-coral-fish-preview.jpg" alt="Fish on Coral Reef" title="Image Source: https://www.pickpik.com/finding-nemo-underwater-sea-coral-fish-nemo-36546" width = "35%" align="right" style="padding: 0 20px;">
 
* Don't be afraid to ask questions, especially about the business. You will have a valuable asset for six months to a year. You will see the business with a fresh set of eyes. You may ask a really good question that no one has thought to ask because they have been embedded in the business domain for too long. It's like two fish who pass a third fish on the reefs. The passing fish asks, "How's the water?" The first fish turns to his friend and asks, "What's water?" They may not be able to see what you can.
* When you run into a technical challenge, don't be afraid to ask for help, but do your due diligence beforehand. Try to find the answer yourself. If stuck, then when asking for help, describe your search effort and ask if there are other places to find the answer before being given the answer. Then if you're still stuck, ask for the answer. Though you want to demonstrate that you put in the effort to find the solution yourself, don't spend too much time on it. That is, don't spend five days being stuck on a problem that could have been answered in five minutes.
* If the question and answer is significant enough and useful to other new people, see if there’s a way for you to document it for others who might run into it. Many places will have an internal Wiki of some type where you might be able to post a question and answer. Ask your mentor where to put it first, since they may have a Q&A repository. This does several things:
    * You’re contributing.
    * It’s a reference for you.
    * It’s a reference for others.
* Don't eat lunch alone at your desk. Use this time to get to know your team and others. This is an excellent way to build and grow your network.
* Have a list of short term, mid term and long term goals. If these aren't provided to you as part of the onboarding process, work with your mentor or manager to come up with a set.
* Keep track of your accomplishments. I usually kept an MS-Word document of bullet listed things I had done. Update it weekly, possibly even daily. It will be a great value when it comes to your annual performance review. I almost always spotted accomplishments while reviewing that I had forgotten about or I had remembered as being in a previous year. If I can't remember my accomplishments, then why should my manager remember them either?
* Schedule a weekly or bi-weekly one-on-one session with your manager if they don't automatically do it. The 1-on-1 isn't about reviewing what you've done. It's more of a conversation with your manager to discuss how things are going, where you see your career going, etc.

# NOTES
o	Data structures and algorithms are like calculator. All function no context. With context they are just "dit boxes". You’ve been learning data structures and algorithms without a business context. With a CS degree,  you understand the inner workings of the worlds biggest and most powerful “calculators”. However, you may not know how to apply that power. A calculator can be used to balance a checkbook, calculate stable orbital trajectories or manage calorie intake for a diet. The power is not in the calculator. The power is knowing how to use the calculator to solve a domain specific problem. Your First job is about learning context, or business domain, and understanding/learning how to apply data structures and algorithms to solve problems in that business domain context. This is a mental paradigm shift, and it takes some time.
 
Here are somethings to consider doing during onboarding and beyond:
•	Invest in your company's 401(k) on day one. Prefer the Roth version, especially early in your career. Invest in a stock market indexed fund of sometype. They will probably offer a Target/Retirement Year fund. That's usually a good option. Invest to reach the company match if you can swing it. Then increase your contribution every time you get a pay raise until you reach the maximum contribution limit. This will take a few years to get there. My wife and I were able to enjoy retirement quite comfortably in our early 60s because we both invested aggressively in our companys' 401(k) plans.

Things will change from your academic career to your business career. The questions will be more broad and less well defined. The answers will be fuzzy. The customer won't be able to describe what they want, but they'll know it when they see it. Or more likely, they tell you that what they see isn't what they wanted. You're not in it alone. You're part of a team all working together. You can help each other. You can look for solutions anywhere.

Use LLMs as much as you can to understand the business and technology, but verify anything it produces. They hallucinate/confabulate results.

Use LLMs to summarize complex online company documents. Use LLMs to give you an understanding of what some legacy code does. However, do not put any company proprietary content, documentation or code, in a generic LLM, such as ChatGPT. We don't know how it might absorb that content and redistribute it. Ask your mentor/manager which LLMs you can safely use. Hopefully, your new employer has a company approved LLM that you can use safely.
