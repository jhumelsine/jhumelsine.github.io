# Lather, Rinse, Repeat
For the most part, just about everything I described in [Intership](TBD) (TBD) applies for your first job as well.
You'll still need a [Résumé](TBD) (TBD), [Elevator Pitch](TBD) (TBD), [Company Search](TBD) (TBD) and [Interviews](TBD) (TBD).

The main differences between obtaining an internship offer and a first job offer are:
* The recruiters and interviewers will be more discerning and challenging. This is no longer a 3-month committment. This is a committment that tends to last for years.
* [Likeability](TBD) (TBD) may be even more important, since you'll definitely be working with a team rather than being sequestered with other interns working on your own projects.
* You may need to put more consideration into your own [Decision](TBD) (TBD), since this is no longer a 3-month committment for you either. You will spend most of your week at work. Make sure you choose based upon attributes important to you, such as: location, company reputation, company culture, compensation, benefits, growth opportunity, etc. Different individuals may consider offers from the same set of companies and reach different conclusion, because people value attributes differently.

The first job is often the most difficult one to get, since most junior software developers have limited practical experience.
Once you have an employment track record with a year or two of practical experience, companies are more willing to consider you as a candidate.

<img src="/assets/RyanKanbanBoard.jpg" alt="Kanban Board"  width = "50%" align="right" style="padding-right: 35px;">

Ideally, most graduates will have a job lined up upon graduation.
But much like the [Alternative Path](TBD) (TBD) situation, which an intership was not obtained, not everyone lands a job before graduation.

My son did not have a job offer when he graduated with his Mechanical Engineering degrees several years ago.
He moved home in the interim, and he and I basically the plan I described in [Finding Your Next Job](https://jhumelsine.github.io/2024/08/11/jobsearch.html#finding-your-next-job), but I put a bit of an [_Agile_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agile_software_development) spin on it via a simple [Kanban Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanban_board) made of Post-It Notes, Sharpies, and a sheet of Costco cardboard.

Most days we reviewed his status and set up a plan for the next day.
I encouraged him to do three things each day to find that first job.
He landed his first job about two months after graduation.

When my son graduated in 2022, his Computer Science classmates were being scooped by companies with wild abandon.
My wife wondered whether he had chosen the wrong major with Mechanical Engineering.
I reassured her that the software industry is cyclical.
As of the time of this blog, four years later, the momentum has shifted.
Generative/Agentic AI is shaking the foundations of software development making that first job upon graduation for Computer Science majors more difficult than other years.
I'm sure the pendulum will swing back once more.
